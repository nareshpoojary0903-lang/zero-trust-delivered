Section 01 Zero Trust, Delivered — A Practitioner's Guide to SASE as Your Implementation Engine

The Thesis

SASE is the delivery mechanism through which Zero Trust becomes enforceable. This section establishes why — and how this guide demonstrates it.

Implementing Zero Trust is not primarily a technology decision. It is an architectural decision about how enforcement infrastructure is organised across every access path in the organisation — user to application, device to resource, data to egress. The principle is well established. The challenge is operational: what delivers the Policy Enforcement Point across web traffic, private application access, SaaS usage, site connectivity, and data egress, at the scale and complexity of a real organisation with a real team and real budget constraints?

Secure Access Service Edge — the convergence of network connectivity and security service delivery into a cloud-native platform — is the answer to that question. SWG, CASB, ZTNA, FWaaS, SSE DLP, and RBI together cover every access path that Zero Trust requires to be governed. These components are not independent capabilities assembled for completeness. Under a unified policy framework, they are the enforcement infrastructure through which Zero Trust becomes operational.

This guide addresses how to implement that unified model — in a mid-size organisation, with a lean security team, against the specific decisions, sequencing challenges, and vendor considerations that practitioners actually face.

What Zero Trust Requires to Function

Zero Trust operates on one principle: no user, device, or connection is trusted by default. Access is granted based on verified identity, assessed device posture, and contextual signals — continuously, not once at login. This principle has been formalised across several frameworks, most notably NIST Special Publication 800-207, which describes the enforcement infrastructure it requires.

NIST SP 800-207 names three functional components that must exist in any Zero Trust architecture: the Policy Decision Point, which evaluates access requests against policy; the Policy Enforcement Point, which acts on those decisions in the traffic path; and the Policy Administrator, which communicates decisions to the enforcement layer. These are not theoretical constructs. They are functional requirements — components that must exist somewhere in the architecture before Zero Trust becomes operational rather than aspirational.

A Zero Trust programme that produces roadmaps, maturity assessments, and architecture diagrams — but does not identify where these components live and how they enforce policy across every access path — has not yet crossed the line between strategy and operation.

The question every organisation must answer is therefore practical: what delivers the Policy Enforcement Point at scale? What intercepts the outbound web session, evaluates the SaaS access request, governs the private application connection, inspects the data leaving the organisation — across the full access surface, for every user and device, regardless of location?

What SASE Provides

SASE converges network connectivity and security service delivery into a cloud-native platform. Its components each address a specific access path — and each maps directly to the Zero Trust enforcement requirement for that path.

SWG is the Policy Enforcement Point for outbound web and internet-bound traffic. CASB is the Policy Enforcement Point for SaaS application access — governing what users do within cloud applications, not just which applications they can reach. ZTNA is the Policy Enforcement Point for private application access, replacing network-level VPN trust with application-level, identity-and-device-verified access. FWaaS provides network-level policy enforcement for all IP traffic, including the site and device populations that SSE components alone cannot govern. SSE DLP governs what data leaves the organisation through the web egress path. RBI enforces execution isolation for access scenarios where the device itself cannot be trusted.

The mapping between Zero Trust pillars and SASE components is structural, not incidental. SASE was not designed to be a Zero Trust platform — it was designed to solve the distributed access problem. But the distributed access problem and the Zero Trust enforcement problem are the same problem, described at different layers of abstraction. The components that solve one solve the other.

The central argument

SASE is not a product you evaluate as an alternative to doing Zero Trust. It is the delivery mechanism through which Zero Trust becomes enforceable. Treating them as separate programmes — Zero Trust as a strategy workstream, SASE as a network transformation — produces a strategy without an enforcement mechanism and a platform without a governing principle. Neither delivers what was promised. Closing that gap is what this guide is about.

A foundational principle — carried through every section of this guide

Every enforcement decision in the unified model is bounded by the quality of the signals available to make it. Identity signals determine the precision of ZTNA and CASB policy decisions. Device posture signals determine the granularity of access control. Data classification signals determine the effectiveness of DLP. Traffic coverage determines whether any control applies to any given session at all. The purpose of the existing investment audit in Section 2 is not only to identify tooling gaps — it is to identify the signal quality gaps that determine how effective deployed controls will actually be.

At this scale, operational simplicity is a security outcome, not a preference. A unified platform the team can fully understand, tune, and respond to during incidents is more resilient than a best-of-breed stack that exceeds the team's operational capacity. Before You Deploy:
The Existing Investment Audit

Establish where you stand before deciding what to build — a structured assessment across maturity and existing investments.

Why the Audit Comes First

The instinct when starting a Zero Trust or SASE programme is to evaluate vendors. That instinct is premature. Vendor capability only matters in relation to what the organisation already has, what it actually needs, and what its team can operationally sustain. Without that context, vendor evaluation becomes a feature comparison — and feature comparison reliably produces the wrong result.

The audit is the instrument that establishes context before vendor conversations begin. It does not produce a procurement list. It produces a gap map — a clear view of where the organisation stands on the Zero Trust journey, what existing investments already cover, what is partially addressed, and where genuine gaps remain. That gap map is the input that makes every subsequent decision rational rather than reactive.

On vendor selection

Every credible SASE vendor has made significant engineering investment in their platform. What varies is fit — how well a vendor's architectural philosophy, deployment model, and capability depth align with the specific risk profile and existing investments of a given organisation. Selection criteria derived from organisational context consistently outperform criteria derived from feature matrices. The audit produces those criteria. The right vendor emerges from the audit — not from the evaluation process that precedes it.

The audit has two layers. The first is a maturity baseline — where does the organisation stand today on the Zero Trust journey, assessed against a recognised framework. The second is an investment assessment — what existing controls and platforms are in place across the five dimensions that SSE deployment depends on. Together they produce the gap map that drives every deployment decision that follows.

Layer 1 — Zero Trust Maturity Baseline

Before assessing existing investments in detail, it is worth establishing where the organisation stands on the Zero Trust journey as a whole. The CISA Zero Trust Maturity Model (ZTMM) v2.0 — the most widely adopted Zero Trust maturity framework — provides a structured baseline for this assessment. It defines four maturity stages across the five Zero Trust pillars: Traditional, Initial, Advanced, and Optimal.

The five pillars of the CISA ZTMM — Identity, Devices, Networks, Applications and Workloads, and Data — map directly to the five audit dimensions in Layer 2, and to the SSE components addressed in Sections 3 through 7 of this guide. Assessing maturity per pillar before SSE deployment establishes the starting position. Re-assessing after key SSE capabilities are deployed demonstrates the movement — which is the programme's measurable security outcome expressed in a recognised framework.

On using the CISA ZTMM

The CISA ZTMM was developed for US federal agencies and its implementation guidance reflects that context. Its maturity stages and pillar definitions, however, are universally applicable. This guide uses the framework as a self-assessment instrument — not as a compliance framework. The full stage descriptions for each pillar are in the CISA ZTMM v2.0 document, available at cisa.gov. This guide references the framework; it does not reproduce it.

Three cross-cutting capabilities span all pillars in the CISA model: Visibility and Analytics, Automation and Orchestration, and Governance. Of these, Governance is addressed directly in Section 10 of this guide as the mechanism that sustains the programme over time.

Layer 2 — The Five Audit Dimensions

Each dimension surfaces a category of existing capability and the gaps that SSE deployment would address. D1 Identity Foundation Primary policy signal for ZTNA and CASB
Key questions to answer
  • Is there a centralised IdP in place — Entra ID, Okta, or equivalent?
  • Is Conditional Access or equivalent policy enforcement actively deployed, not merely configured?
  • Does SSO coverage extend across both SaaS and private applications, or are significant applications still using local or legacy authentication?
  • Is MFA enforced broadly across the user population, or selectively for specific roles?
  • Are service accounts and non-human identities inventoried and governed?
Displacement signals
  • Entra ID with mature Conditional Access and broad SSO coverage means the identity layer is ready — ZTNA and CASB will build on a strong foundation. The enforcement quality of every subsequent component is proportional to this foundation.
  • Applications outside SSO represent a prerequisite workstream. Extending SSO coverage before SSE components that depend on identity signals are deployed improves policy quality across every enforcement decision.
  • Weak or selectively enforced MFA reduces identity signal quality — the enforcement quality of every control that depends on verified identity is proportionally reduced.
D2 Endpoint and Device Estate Determines ZTNA architecture and access path design
Key questions to answer
  • What is the split between managed (corporate-owned) and unmanaged (personal, contractor, third-party) devices?
  • Is MDM or UEM deployed — Microsoft Intune, JAMF, or equivalent — with active compliance policy enforcement?
  • Is device compliance status available as a signal that access policy can consume in real time?
  • Is Endpoint DLP deployed and tuned to the organisation's sensitive data categories?
  • Is EDR or XDR in place, providing active endpoint visibility and response capability?

  • If Endpoint DLP is deployed and tuned, SSE DLP addresses residual browser-based egress risk rather than primary data protection — assess what browser upload paths the Endpoint DLP specifically covers before adding SSE-layer DLP.

Key questions to answer

Secure Web Gateway

Zero Trust enforcement for internet-bound and outbound web traffic — the Policy Enforcement Point where every user connection to the internet begins.

ZT Pillar coverage: Network · Application · Data

Returning to the reference table in Section 1: SWG is the Policy Enforcement Point for outbound web and internet-bound traffic. Every connection a user initiates toward an internet destination — a website, a web application, a cloud service — passes through this enforcement layer. Without it, those connections are ungoverned. The organisation has no visibility into what leaves, what arrives, or what executes in the browser.

That enforcement role does not require the most sophisticated deployment in the SSE stack. But it does require deliberate decisions — about what traffic to intercept, how to inspect it, and what the enforcement layer can and cannot do. Those decisions are the substance of this section.

DNS Filtering: The Foundation Layer

Before examining how SWG operates, it is worth establishing the control that belongs beneath it in the enforcement stack. DNS filtering operates at the resolution layer — before a TCP connection is established, before TLS is negotiated, before any traffic inspection is possible. When a device requests resolution of a known-malicious domain, that request is blocked. No connection is attempted. Nothing reaches the endpoint.

Foundation control

DNS Filtering — Scope and Limits

DNS filtering blocks connections to known-malicious domains at the resolution layer. No decryption is required. No traffic inspection overhead. It is the lightest-weight enforcement control that produces meaningful, immediate threat reduction — and the right starting point for a mid-size organisation that does not yet have SWG in place.

What it addresses
  • Known-malicious domain connections
  • Command-and-control callback blocking
  • Malware distribution domain blocking
  • Phishing domain blocking
  • DNS tunnelling detection
What it cannot address
  • Content inspection within permitted sessions
  • TLS/SSL visibility
  • DLP enforcement on uploads or downloads
  • Application-layer policy enforcement
  • New or uncategorised malicious domains

DNS filtering and SWG are complementary — not alternatives. DNS filtering remains active after SWG is deployed. It provides the fastest possible threat response (at the DNS resolution layer, before any connection is established) while SWG provides depth (content inspection, DLP, application-layer policy). Many SSE platforms include DNS filtering as part of the same enforcement stack, where it operates as Stage 1 of the traffic flow described above rather than as a separate product.

How SWG Enforces: The Three Policy Signals

SWG enforcement operates across three distinct policy signals, each adding a layer of precision to the access decision.

Identity signals

Who is making the connection? SWG policy can be scoped to specific users, groups, or roles — applying stricter controls to sensitive populations. Identity signals from the IdP integration are what make SWG Zero Trust-aware rather than network-based.

Device posture signals

What device is generating the traffic? Managed devices with confirmed compliance posture can be granted access to a broader URL surface. Unmanaged or non-compliant devices receive more restrictive policy or are routed to RBI isolation. Signal quality is bounded by MDM/UEM integration quality.

Content signals

What is the traffic content? URL categorisation, file inspection, DLP pattern matching, and threat intelligence all operate against the decrypted content stream. Content signals require TLS inspection to be active — without decryption, SWG enforcement is limited to URL and domain-level decisions.

The Decryption Decision

SWG's content inspection capabilities — malware scanning, DLP, application-layer control — depend on TLS decryption. Without it, SWG can only make decisions based on the destination URL and domain reputation. The decryption decision is not a binary choice between inspect-everything and inspect-nothing. It is a governed categorisation — different traffic types receive different decryption treatment based on their risk and privacy profile.

Bypass list governance — periodic review, documented rationale for each entry, a named owner — is an operational discipline that belongs in the deployment plan from day one. This is a specific instance of the governance function addressed in Section 10 — the bypass list is a category of exception that requires the same documented justification, named owner, expiry date, and periodic review that the governance framework prescribes for all Zero Trust exceptions.

The cloud delivery trust boundary

In cloud-delivered SWG, TLS inspection occurs at the vendor's infrastructure. The vendor's platform terminates the TLS session from the client, inspects the cleartext content, and initiates a new TLS session to the destination. In well-architected platforms, that cleartext exposure is transient — content exists in volatile memory during inspection and is not written to persistent storage.

However, the assurance that this is so rests on a trust relationship with the vendor, not on a control the customer can directly verify. Vendor certifications — SOC 2 Type II and ISO 27001 — establish that the vendor maintains a security programme. They do not provide specific assurance about the inspection pipeline, memory handling practices, or administrator access controls for traffic processing infrastructure. Reviewing what is specifically covered in a vendor's SOC 2 report — not just that the certification exists — is the minimum due diligence step.

Regulatory due diligence

Deploying cloud SSE for all-traffic TLS inspection is structurally different from onboarding an individual SaaS application. The scope of personal data processed by the vendor extends to every HTTPS session from every user. This may trigger data protection obligations — including a Data Protection Impact Assessment under GDPR Article 35, works council consultation in certain European jurisdictions, and employee notification requirements. Organisations with existing GDPR compliance programmes should extend their standard DPA and DPIA process to cover this deployment. Organisations without a formal GDPR programme should engage legal counsel before deploying TLS inspection at scale.

This is not a reason to avoid cloud SSE. It is a reason to sequence legal and compliance due diligence correctly — before deployment, not after.

Malware Inspection and Threat Detection

SWG's threat detection operates as a three-layer funnel. The design is deliberate: the most computationally expensive inspection — sandboxing — is reserved for the smallest proportion of traffic. The vast majority of threats are identified and blocked at the cheaper, faster layers before sandboxing is needed.

Three-layer malware inspection funnel — all traffic enters Layer 1; only suspicious traffic proceeds to Layer 3
LAYER 1
Signature & Reputation

The fast path. File hashes checked against continuously updated threat intelligence databases. Known-malicious files blocked at the hash level before deeper inspection is required. URL and domain reputation scoring provides a parallel fast-path filter for the connection itself. Computationally cheap. Catches the majority of known malware. Most traffic is cleared or blocked at this layer.

LAYER 2
Static Analysis

Pre-execution inspection. For files passing the reputation layer but not positively identified as safe: examination of file structure, embedded code, macros, and scripts without executing them. Catches malware modified to evade hash-based detection — obfuscated JavaScript, macro-based attacks, polyglot files. Deobfuscation and script unpacking are part of this layer. A meaningful fraction of sophisticated threats are caught here before execution is required.

LAYER 3
Sandboxing

Dynamic analysis. Files remaining suspicious after Layers 1 and 2 are executed in an isolated cloud container. Behavioural signals observed: file system activity, network calls, process creation, registry modifications. Most effective for zero-day and advanced threats. Most compute-intensive and highest latency.

Honest limitations: In non-blocking mode (the most common production configuration), files are delivered while analysis completes — if found malicious, the file is retroactively blocked. Sandboxing in non-blocking mode is detective rather than fully preventive. Sophisticated malware can detect sandbox environments and delay malicious behaviour — this remains an active arms race. Encrypted traffic without TLS decryption cannot be sandboxed.

When evaluating SSE vendors, confirm that all three inspection layers are supported and active. Verify which layer handles which traffic volume and what the latency impact is for Layer 3 in the expected traffic profile.

Protocol Evolution and the Limits of Inspection

SWG enforcement operates on TLS-over-TCP traffic. HTTP/3 moves web traffic to QUIC — a transport protocol running over UDP with encryption integrated more deeply into the transport layer. Most SSE vendors currently address this by blocking QUIC traffic, forcing the client to fall back to TCP-based TLS connections that remain inspectable. For most SaaS applications today, that fallback is graceful and transparent to the user.

As QUIC adoption deepens across CDN infrastructure and custom applications, the proportion of traffic that can be cleanly forced to downgrade will decrease. This reinforces the importance of the unified model — enforcement layers that do not depend on traffic decryption become proportionally more important as inspection becomes harder to sustain at the network layer. For a mid-size organisation today, this is a forward signal, not an immediate operational challenge.

What SWG Cannot Do Alone

SWG enforces policy on traffic it can see and inspect. Three categories of risk sit outside that boundary.

  • SaaS application governance at depth

    SWG can block or allow a SaaS destination. It cannot see inside a SaaS application — which files are being shared externally, which OAuth permissions have been granted, which data is being moved between tenants. That visibility and governance is CASB territory. SWG governs the path to the SaaS application; CASB governs what happens within it.

  • Private application access control

    SWG handles outbound internet-bound traffic. It has no role in governing access to internal or privately hosted applications. That access control is ZTNA territory. An organisation that deploys SWG without ZTNA has governed its internet egress but left its private application access on a network-trust model.

  • Endpoint-resident and lateral threats

    Malware executing entirely on the endpoint, credentials stolen before a connection is initiated, and lateral movement within the network are not visible to SWG. The traffic SWG inspects is what leaves the endpoint toward the internet — not what happens on the endpoint before that point, and not east-west traffic between internal systems. Endpoint security (EDR/XDR) and network segmentation address these threat surfaces.

Cloud Access Security Broker

Visibility and governance for SaaS applications — the enforcement layer that picks up precisely where SWG ends.

ZT Pillar coverage: Identity · Application · Data

SWG governs whether a user can reach a SaaS application. CASB governs what happens inside it. These are not overlapping concerns — they are sequential enforcement layers that together close the visibility gap that SWG leaves behind the application boundary.

The distinction matters practically. A user who authenticates to a corporate Microsoft 365 tenant, accesses a SharePoint document library, and shares its contents externally has passed through every SWG control with no violation. The SWG saw a valid authenticated session to a permitted SaaS destination and allowed it. CASB sees the sharing action within the application — and that is where the governance failure occurs if CASB is not in place.

Two Deployment Models: Inline and API

CASB operates in two distinct modes that address different risk surfaces and require different deployment architectures. Most production CASB deployments use both — they are complementary, not alternatives.

Inline CASB

Real-time enforcement · Requires traffic steering
  • Sits in the traffic path between user and SaaS application
  • Real-time access control and data governance decisions
  • Tenant awareness enforcement — blocks personal account access
  • Upload controls for sanctioned and unsanctioned applications
  • Inline DLP for data matching sensitive content criteria
  • Shadow IT discovery from observed traffic patterns
  • Requires SSE agent deployed and traffic steered through SSE platform
Dependent on the SWG traffic path — inline CASB and SWG operate within the same enforcement engine in a unified SSE platform.

API CASB

Retrospective governance · Direct SaaS API integration
  • Connects directly to SaaS platforms via their native APIs
  • Scans data at rest within SaaS applications
  • Assesses sharing settings, permissions, and OAuth grants
  • Identifies policy violations that occurred before CASB was deployed
  • Malware scanning for files stored in corporate SaaS platforms
  • Operates independently of traffic steering — can start before SSE agent is deployed
  • Covers desktop sync clients and integration paths outside browser sessions
API access governed by SaaS platform API availability — not all platforms offer the same API depth or grant equivalent access.

The Correct Deployment Sequence

The most common CASB deployment failure is activating enforcement before completing discovery. Organisations that move directly to enforcement — blocking, alerting, restricting — without first understanding the actual SaaS footprint produce policies that over-block legitimate use and under-block actual risk. The correct sequence is discovery, then governance, then enforcement.

Correct sequence
1
Discover — Enable API CASB and SWG observation mode. Build the actual SaaS footprint picture. Understand what applications exist, how they are used, and what data moves where. Run for at least two to four weeks before any enforcement decision.
2
Govern — Classify the footprint. Sanctioned, tolerated, blocked. Configure tenant awareness for priority platforms. Assess OAuth grants. Understand sharing policies for critical SaaS applications.
3
Enforce — Activate upload controls, DLP policies, and application-level restrictions against a known, classified footprint. Enforcement is now calibrated to what actually exists — not to what was assumed.
Enforcement is precise because it is built on real visibility. False positives are low because the footprint is understood before policy is activated.
Common failure pattern
1
Enforce immediately — Activate blocking policies from day one based on assumed risk categories rather than observed traffic. Block all "file sharing" or "personal cloud storage" categories wholesale.
2
Receive complaints — Legitimate business applications blocked. Approved vendor portals inaccessible. Workflow integrations broken. Security team spends the first weeks responding to escalations rather than configuring policy.
3
Add exceptions — Exceptions accumulate as each blocked application is escalated. Policy drift begins immediately. The exception list becomes the effective policy within weeks.
Enforcement is imprecise, business trust in the security programme is damaged, and the exception list grows faster than the policy set.

Tenant Awareness: The Foundational CASB Control

Tenant awareness — the ability to distinguish between a corporate SaaS tenant and a personal account on the same platform — is the highest-value, lowest-complexity CASB enforcement action available to most organisations. It requires no data classification. It produces immediate and meaningful risk reduction. And it is scoped to a clearly defined risk: access to personal cloud accounts from corporate devices using the same platform the organisation uses for business.

The risk is specific: a user who logs into their personal OneDrive, Google Drive, or Dropbox account from a corporate device has an ungoverned data transfer channel. The SWG sees a permitted destination. The tenant boundary is invisible to network-layer controls. Tenant awareness makes that boundary enforceable.

Early deployment value

Tenant awareness is often the most immediately deployable and highest-value CASB capability for an organisation early in its SSE programme. It does not require data classification to be in place. It does not require a mature DLP policy. It requires knowing which SaaS platforms have both corporate and personal tenant variants — which discovery mode surfaces — and configuring tenant restriction policy for those platforms. This makes it a sound early win: meaningful risk reduction, low operational complexity, deployable before the broader CASB enforcement programme is fully configured.

How tenant awareness is enforced — three mechanisms

SSE vendors implement tenant awareness through three distinct technical mechanisms. The applicable mechanism depends on the SaaS platform's architecture and the SSE platform's inspection capability.

MECHANISM 1
HTTP Header Injection

When a user authenticates to a SaaS platform, the SSE platform intercepts the authentication request and injects specific HTTP headers before forwarding to the SaaS authentication endpoint. For Microsoft 365, the Restrict-Access-To-Tenants and Restrict-Access-Context headers instruct Microsoft's authentication service to permit corporate tenant login only — blocking personal account access at Microsoft's server side. Requires TLS inspection active. Enforces at the authentication boundary — prevents personal tenant sessions from being established rather than detecting them after the fact.

Requires the SaaS platform's authentication endpoint to honour the restriction headers. Microsoft 365 has the deepest support. Coverage varies significantly across other SaaS platforms.
MECHANISM 2
Session-Level Identity Inspection

The SSE platform inspects active session content inline — API calls, JSON payloads, cookie headers, URL structures — to determine which tenant or instance the established session is associated with. Does not require the SaaS platform to use a specific IdP or support header-based restriction protocols. Works wherever the SaaS application's traffic carries recognisable tenant or identity signals. Broader applicability than header injection — extends tenant awareness beyond the Microsoft ecosystem to other major SaaS platforms. Requires TLS inspection active.

Less effective for SaaS applications whose session traffic is fully opaque to tenant identification — no domain, no tenant ID, no recognisable signal in the traffic.
MECHANISM 3
Authentication Redirection

The SSE platform intercepts the user's initial request to the SaaS application and redirects the authentication flow through its reverse proxy. The CASB validates identity before allowing the session to reach the SaaS application — inserting a corporate identity verification step regardless of which identity store the SaaS platform uses natively. Works for browser-based authentication flows where the authentication path can be cleanly intercepted.

Less applicable for mobile apps with certificate pinning, thick clients that do not use browser authentication flows, or APIs with direct credential validation.
Practical guidance

Header injection is the strongest preventive control for Microsoft 365 and compatible platforms. Session-level inspection provides the widest applicability across the broader SaaS estate without requiring per-application IdP integration. In a mature CASB deployment, the first two mechanisms are used in combination. For SaaS applications whose traffic carries no recognisable tenant signals and whose authentication flow cannot be intercepted, access control falls back to URL-level blocking or application categorisation. Verify which mechanisms the shortlisted vendor supports for the specific SaaS applications in the organisation's footprint before committing to a deployment design that assumes universal tenant awareness.

SaaS Data Risk: Ten Scenarios CASB Addresses

Rather than an abstract capability inventory, the following scenarios illustrate where CASB enforcement produces concrete outcomes. Each maps to capabilities described above.

Personal tenant exfiltration

Inline CASB · Tenant awareness

Corporate data moved from a sanctioned SaaS application to a personal cloud storage account — same platform, different tenant. Invisible to network-layer controls. Tenant awareness enforcement prevents access to personal tenants from corporate devices, and upload controls block transfer of content matching sensitive data policies to unsanctioned destinations.

Oversharing via link

API CASB · Sharing governance

Sensitive files shared internally via "anyone with the link" settings — accessible to anyone who receives or discovers the link, with no authentication requirement. API CASB continuously assesses sharing settings, identifies files with overly broad access, and can automatically remediate by converting public links to restricted access or alerting the file owner.

OAuth application abuse

API CASB · OAuth governance

Users granting third-party applications access to corporate SaaS data through OAuth consent — often without awareness of the scope of access being granted. A productivity tool, browser extension, or integration granted read/write access to corporate email or file storage becomes an unmanaged access path. API CASB provides visibility into OAuth grants and enables revocation of grants that exceed acceptable risk thresholds.

Shadow AI data exposure

Inline CASB · SWG category control

Users uploading corporate documents to consumer AI platforms — summarisation tools, writing assistants, analysis services — where document content becomes input to a third-party model with terms of service that may permit training on submitted content. Inline CASB upload controls for AI platform categories, combined with SWG category-based controls as a first layer, provide the enforcement mechanism. One of the fastest-growing ungoverned data exposure vectors in mid-size organisations.

SaaS-to-SaaS data movement

API CASB · Inline CASB

Data moved between sanctioned and unsanctioned applications through platform integrations — workflow automation tools, iPaaS connectors, and native integrations that transfer data between SaaS platforms without passing through a browser. API CASB provides visibility into connected application integrations and their data access scope. Inline CASB addresses browser-based transfer paths. Understanding the specific integration landscape is part of the discovery phase.

Excessive SaaS permissions

API CASB · Permission governance

Users granted owner or admin rights to SaaS instances — Salesforce objects, SharePoint sites, Google Workspace shared drives — that exceed what their role requires. API CASB audits permission assignments across the SaaS estate, identifies over-permissioned accounts, and enables remediation. Least-privilege at the SaaS application layer is a governance discipline CASB can enforce systematically rather than through periodic manual review.

Data sovereignty concerns

Inline CASB · Upload destination governance

Sensitive data uploaded to SaaS tenants whose data processing location does not align with the organisation's regulatory obligations. Inline CASB upload destination governance prevents uploads to tenants whose processing geography is outside the required boundary. Important boundary: CASB governs which SaaS tenant receives the data — the SaaS vendor's own data residency configuration governs where that vendor physically stores data within their own infrastructure.

Anomalous account behaviour

CASB behavioural analytics

A user account exhibiting patterns inconsistent with their baseline — unusual geographic origin, atypical access time, abnormal data download volume. Behavioural CASB analytics identify these patterns as indicators of compromised accounts. The detection signal is anomaly from baseline rather than a specific prohibited action. Requires a baselining period — typically two to four weeks — before anomaly detection becomes effective.

Malware via SaaS storage

API CASB · File scanning at rest

A user synchronises a malware-infected file to corporate SharePoint or OneDrive. Other users with access to the same location download the infected file. API CASB scans files at rest in SaaS platforms for malware and quarantines infected files before they spread. This threat vector is entirely outside the SWG inspection path — the file entered through a sync client and spread through legitimate SaaS sharing mechanisms.

Third-party application abuse

API CASB · OAuth governance (ongoing)

A third-party application granted OAuth access to corporate SaaS data begins behaving anomalously after being compromised. The initial grant was approved — the behaviour change is what creates the risk. API CASB monitors whether integrated applications continue to operate within expected parameters over time, not just at the point of initial access grant. Ongoing OAuth governance, not just initial grant assessment.

What CASB Cannot Do Alone

  • Identity attacks and compromised sessions

    CASB sees authenticated sessions. It does not see how authentication was compromised. A session established through credential theft, MFA bypass, or token hijacking appears legitimate to CASB. Identity security controls at the IdP layer — anomaly detection, risk-based conditional access, phishing-resistant MFA — address the upstream problem that CASB cannot.

  • Endpoint compromise

    CASB governs the SaaS session of whoever is authenticated on the device. If the endpoint is compromised before the session is initiated, CASB enforces policy on a session whose legitimacy is undermined at the device layer. Behavioural anomaly detection within CASB may surface unusual patterns — but CASB is not an endpoint security control. EDR and XDR address endpoint compromise.

  • Non-web and non-API access paths

    CASB covers SaaS access through web browsers and SaaS APIs. It does not cover data movement through desktop sync clients operating outside the browser path, email attachments sent directly from mail clients, or removable media. Those access paths require endpoint DLP and email security controls. CASB is one layer in a complementary set of data protection controls — not a comprehensive data loss prevention programme on its own.

ZTNA and Firewall as a Service

Zero Trust enforcement for private application access and network-level traffic policy — the two controls that replace VPN and perimeter firewall in the SASE model.

ZT Pillar coverage: Identity · Device · Network · Application

SASE delivers policy enforcement through a combination of cloud PoPs and on-premises nodes — not exclusively from the cloud. Cloud PoPs handle remote user and internet-bound traffic; on-premises nodes address branch connectivity, data centre integration, OT/IoT segmentation, and scenarios where cloud routing introduces unacceptable latency or data protection constraints. SD-WAN appliances deployed at branch offices or data centres are on-premises manifestations of the SASE policy enforcement layer — managed centrally through the cloud management plane but executing policy locally.

SWG governs internet-bound traffic. CASB governs SaaS application usage. Neither governs access to private applications — internal systems, on-premises infrastructure, or privately hosted services. And neither addresses network-level traffic policy for sites, branches, and device populations that do not traverse the SSE enforcement path.

Two components close those gaps. ZTNA replaces VPN as the access control mechanism for private applications — enforcing identity and device-based access at the application level rather than granting network-level trust. FWaaS replaces the perimeter and branch office firewall — delivering network-level policy enforcement from the cloud rather than from on-premises appliances. They operate at different layers of the same Zero Trust network pillar, and in a full SASE deployment they work together.

Managing the VPN to ZTNA Migration

In a modern SSE deployment, the endpoint agent that steers traffic to the SSE platform — for SWG enforcement, for CASB inline visibility, for DLP — is the ZTNA agent. They are the same component. The agent establishes the secure tunnel to the SSE platform and handles all policy-enforced traffic routing from the endpoint. Deploying a separate VPN client alongside it means two tunnel stacks on the same device competing for traffic routing decisions.

The practical consequences are real and consistent: split DNS configurations that cause resolution failures when both clients are active, conflicting routing tables that produce unpredictable traffic paths, policy enforcement gaps where traffic falls outside both enforcement domains, and a user experience degradation that generates support escalations and pressure to create exceptions that erode the security model. Permanent VPN and SSE agent coexistence is operationally unsound — not because a brief transition period is impossible, but because running both indefinitely as a permanent architecture produces degradation that accumulates over time.

The SSE deployment and the VPN migration are the same programme event. The question is not whether to run both in parallel indefinitely — it is how to migrate private application access from VPN to ZTNA, application by application, in a bounded transition period. Section 8 addresses the migration sequence in detail — specifically Step 0 (steering configuration) and Step 5 (application-by-application VPN decommission). The parallel operation period is planned and bounded, not open-ended.

ZTNA as More Than VPN Replacement

Organisations typically adopt ZTNA for one of two reasons: to replace ageing VPN infrastructure, or to eliminate the VPN hardware refresh cost. Both are legitimate motivations. Neither is sufficient as the primary design objective for a Zero Trust programme.

The pattern that emerges in practice is this: the SSE platform authenticates the user, and then grants access in a manner that closely resembles what VPN provided — a broad network-level path, or access to a wide set of applications, scoped loosely by role. The authentication mechanism is stronger. The delivery infrastructure is cloud-native. But the access model is unchanged. The user can reach everything they could reach on VPN, delivered through a more modern mechanism.

The Zero Trust principle gap — and the recommendation

NIST SP 800-207 is explicit on what Zero Trust access policy requires: access decisions are made per subject, per resource, and per session — not per user group per network segment. The policy engine evaluates identity, device posture, and context for each access request. Access is scoped to the specific application or resource requested, not to the network that contains it. Least-privilege is enforced at the application level, not assumed at the network level.

Most ZTNA deployments, as implemented, do not reach this standard. They deliver authenticated access — which is a meaningful improvement over implicit network trust — but stop short of application-scoped, least-privilege, continuously evaluated access policy. The result is a platform with Zero Trust capability that is operated as a better VPN.

The recommendation: Use the ZTNA deployment as the deliberate opportunity to implement application-scoped, least-privilege access policy from the start. This requires upfront investment in application inventory, access policy design, and identity governance — all of which Dimension 3 and Dimension 1 of the existing investment audit address as prerequisites. The cost of building least-privilege policy at deployment is significantly lower than retrofitting it onto a ZTNA deployment already operating as a VPN replacement.

The Enclave Gateway Model

Before examining how ZTNA connectivity architectures differ, it is worth establishing the conceptual model that underpins why ZTNA can protect private applications without requiring those applications to be modified. NIST SP 800-207 describes this as the Enclave Gateway Model — and it is the architectural pattern that the ZTNA connector or broker component implements in practice.

An enclave, in this context, is a defined network segment containing one or more private resources — a set of application servers in a data centre, a workload in a cloud VPC, a group of services in a hybrid environment. Rather than placing a policy enforcement point on every individual resource within the enclave, the Enclave Gateway Model places a single enforcement component at the boundary of that segment. All access to resources within the enclave flows through this gateway. The gateway enforces Zero Trust policy on behalf of every resource it protects.

Why the enclave gateway model matters

The enclave gateway resolves one of the most practical obstacles in Zero Trust adoption: legacy applications cannot be modified to participate in a Zero Trust architecture. They were not built to authenticate against an IdP, evaluate device posture, or enforce per-session access policy. The enclave gateway absorbs that responsibility at the boundary. The application continues to trust what reaches it through the gateway — and the gateway ensures that only verified, policy-permitted access does so.

This is the architectural justification for deploying a ZTNA connector or broker close to private applications. The connector is the enclave gateway — it sits at the boundary of the private application environment, establishes an outbound connection to the ZTNA enforcement platform, and makes applications reachable only through that governed path. No inbound connections from the internet are required. No firewall ports are exposed. The application attack surface is reduced to the connector boundary, and access through that boundary is governed by Zero Trust policy.

The enclave concept also explains the security improvement over VPN at the network level. VPN grants access to the network segment — once inside, a user can reach any resource on that segment. The enclave gateway grants access to specific resources within the segment, through the gateway, with policy enforced at every access request. Lateral movement from a compromised session to adjacent resources within the enclave is not possible through the ZTNA access path.

The Connectivity Architecture Decision

ZTNA is not a single connectivity model. Five distinct access path architectures exist — understanding them is essential before selecting a vendor or designing a migration from VPN. The application estate audit from Section 2 Dimension 3 provides the inputs this decision requires.

Method 1 — Proxy-based ZTNA

Application-layer enforcement
How it works
The SSE platform terminates the client connection and initiates a new connection to the application. Traffic is proxied at the application layer — full visibility into the session.
Strengths
Rich application-layer policy enforcement. Works cleanly for all web-fronted HTTPS applications. Identity and device posture evaluated before the application connection is established.
Cannot carry
Non-HTTP protocols, UDP-heavy applications, multicast, domain controller communication, SMB file shares, print services, VoIP and video with strict latency requirements.
Best for
Modern web-fronted private applications, cloud-hosted internal services, development environments accessible over HTTPS.

Method 2 — Tunnel-based ZTNA

Network-layer routed micro-connection
How it works
A lightweight network tunnel creates a scoped, routed micro-connection to a specific application or resource. The user gets a defined network path — not full VPN network access.
Strengths
Broader protocol support — non-HTTP, UDP, multicast, legacy applications, and network-dependent services all traverse the tunnel without modification.
Trade-off
Application-layer policy enforcement is thinner — less visibility into traffic content compared to proxy-based model. Closer to VPN in enforcement depth.
Best for
Legacy on-premises applications, ERP systems with non-HTTP access patterns, domain services, file shares, and any private application with protocol dependencies beyond HTTPS.

Method 3 — Clientless ZTNA / Application Portal

NIST 800-207 Resource Portal-Based Deployment · Unmanaged devices · No agent required

After IdP-based authentication, the user lands on a browser-based portal displaying only the specific applications they are permitted to access. Each application launches directly in the browser — proxied through the ZTNA connector to the internal resource. No agent, no client, no device onboarding required.

This access model corresponds to the Resource Portal-Based Deployment described in NIST SP 800-207 — one of the framework's three foundational ZTNA deployment models. The portal acts as the Policy Enforcement Point; the user's device has no direct connection to the resource. Supported protocols typically include HTTPS, RDP over HTTPS, VNC, and SSH. Non-web and non-standard protocol applications sit outside the portal model's reach — tunnel-based ZTNA or a separate access path is required for those.

Most SSE vendors provide this capability. Verify protocol support against the private application estate before deployment.

Method 4 — Service Token / mTLS

Non-human access · Automated processes · IoT and API subjects

For automated services, APIs, and IoT devices — subjects that cannot authenticate through an IdP flow — service token or mutual TLS certificate provides the authentication mechanism. Access is scoped to specific resources as with agent-based ZTNA, but the identity credential is a service token or client certificate rather than a user identity assertion. This connects to the workload identity discussion in Section 10 — the same least-privilege access principle applies to automated subjects as to human users.

Workload identity at full depth extends beyond SSE into cloud-native IAM controls — addressed in Section 10.

Method 5 — Network-level ZTNA

Last resort · Legacy protocols · Broadest protocol compatibility

IP-level access for legacy protocols that no other ZTNA model can carry — proprietary industrial protocols, raw network-dependent applications, and any access pattern where application-level proxy or tunnel-scoped connectivity is not feasible. Operated at the IP network layer rather than the application layer. Broadest protocol compatibility but least application-level policy granularity. Often combined with SD-WAN and FWaaS for network-level enforcement where ZTNA application-layer policy cannot apply.

The least-preferred model — use only when Methods 1–4 are genuinely inapplicable to the access pattern in question.
Before selecting a vendor

A vendor whose ZTNA implementation is proxy-only will leave a set of access patterns that cannot migrate from VPN. Identifying non-HTTP and network-dependent access patterns during the application estate audit, before vendor selection, is the instrument that prevents this. Most mid-size organisations have both pattern types in their private application estate. Verify that the shortlisted vendor supports both proxy-based and tunnel-based connectivity — or has a documented answer for the access patterns that proxy-based ZTNA cannot carry. Also verify: do you have significant non-HTTP protocol dependencies in your private application estate? If yes, proxy-only vendors are disqualified for that scenario.

Identity and Device Posture: Both Signals Required

ZTNA without device posture is a materially weaker control than the Zero Trust model requires. Identity confirms who is requesting access. Device posture confirms what they are requesting it from. Without both signals, the policy engine is operating with incomplete information.

A user with valid credentials accessing a sensitive application from an unmanaged personal device — no endpoint security, no disk encryption, potentially compromised — represents a fundamentally different risk than the same user on a managed, compliant corporate device. Without device posture as a policy signal, ZTNA grants or denies access based on identity alone. That is better than VPN's implicit network trust model. It is not Zero Trust access control in the sense that NIST SP 800-207 describes.

The practical requirement: ZTNA policy should express access decisions as a function of both identity and device posture, continuously evaluated per session. The managed versus unmanaged device split identified in Dimension 2 of the existing investment audit determines which user populations can receive full identity-and-device policy enforcement, and which require a different access path.

Beyond Initial Deployment: Trust Algorithm and Continuous Access Evaluation

These concepts define where the programme goes after initial ZTNA deployment is stable. They map to the Advanced and Optimal stages of the CISA ZTMM and require mature identity, device, and behavioural signal infrastructure before they become operational rather than theoretical.

Trust Algorithm (NIST SP 800-207 Section 3.3). At basic maturity, the policy engine's access decision is binary — authenticated or not, compliant device or not. NIST SP 800-207 Section 3.3 describes a more capable form: score-based and contextual. The confidence level adjusts based on the combination of identity assurance, device posture, location, time of day, behavioural patterns, and threat intelligence signals. A lower confidence score triggers step-up authentication or restricts access to lower-sensitivity resources rather than making a binary permit/deny decision. This is what makes Zero Trust genuinely dynamic rather than a more sophisticated perimeter.

Continuous Access Evaluation (CAE). Where traditional access control evaluates trust once at session establishment, CAE re-evaluates trust signals throughout the session. If a signal changes mid-session — device falls out of compliance, threat intelligence flags the source IP, user behaviour becomes anomalous — CAE can restrict or revoke access without requiring a new authentication event. In SSE/ZTNA platforms, CAE manifests as: device posture re-evaluated at intervals during a session, Conditional Access risk signals triggering session restriction, and behavioural anomaly detection causing step-down of access scope.

Firewall as a Service: The Network-Layer Complement

ZTNA controls application-level access for users and workloads — who gets to which application, under what conditions. FWaaS enforces network-level policy for all IP traffic — what traffic is permitted between network segments, sites, and the internet. They address different layers of the same Zero Trust network pillar and are complementary in a complete SASE deployment.

ZTNA does not replace the perimeter firewall. It replaces the access control function that VPN served for user-to-application connectivity. The perimeter firewall governs a broader traffic surface — site-to-site connectivity, inbound traffic from the internet, east-west traffic between network segments, and all IP protocols including those that ZTNA and SWG do not handle. FWaaS is the cloud-delivered mechanism that replaces that function.

Layer 3–7 policy enforcement

Handles all IP traffic — web and non-web protocols, UDP, ICMP, and application traffic that does not traverse the SWG or ZTNA enforcement path.

Branch and site policy

Enforces consistent security policy for traffic originating from branch offices and physical sites — without requiring local firewall appliances at each location.

OT/IoT segmentation

Enforces network-level isolation between OT/IoT device segments and corporate user networks. OT devices can communicate with permitted management systems while lateral movement to user segments is prevented at the network layer.

Centralised policy management

Security policy is defined and managed centrally in the SASE platform and enforced consistently across all sites — eliminating the per-site policy drift that distributed firewall appliances accumulate over time.

The OT/IoT segmentation connection

The OT/IoT device populations identified in Dimension 5 of the existing investment audit find their primary security control in the FWaaS layer. OT and IoT devices cannot run endpoint agents. They cannot authenticate through an IdP. ZTNA's application-level access model does not apply to them. The control available at the SASE layer is network segmentation — enforced through FWaaS policy that defines what these devices can communicate with and explicitly prevents everything else.

SD-WAN provides the connectivity fabric for site and device traffic. FWaaS provides the policy enforcement layer that governs that fabric. Together, they address the network-layer Zero Trust requirement for environments that SSE controls alone cannot govern. This is why the OT/IoT trigger condition identified in the audit accelerates the SD-WAN and FWaaS components from future milestone to current programme requirement for organisations where that population is significant.

The global private backbone and WAN transformation

Strategic consideration — vendor selection input

Some SASE vendors operate a global private backbone — a network of interconnected PoPs that carries customer traffic between sites over the vendor's own private infrastructure rather than the public internet. For organisations with distributed branch office footprints and MPLS contracts approaching renewal, this architecture changes the FWaaS conversation from a security decision to a network transformation and cost optimisation decision with security embedded. MPLS circuits are expensive, carry long contract terms, and are operationally rigid. A SASE platform with a private backbone can provide comparable performance characteristics at substantially lower cost — and the security enforcement comes with the connectivity, not as a separate programme.

This capability is not universal across SASE vendors. Whether a vendor operates a private backbone, and the geographic coverage of that backbone relative to the organisation's site footprint, is a concrete evaluation criterion for organisations where WAN transformation is in scope. It is flagged here as a selection input — Section 9 addresses how to weight it.

FWaaS and the hardware refresh question

Organisations with existing next-generation firewall deployments have invested significantly in on-premises capability — policy sets, rule bases, operational processes, and engineering expertise built around those platforms. FWaaS is the architectural destination as those investments age and hardware refresh cycles arrive. It is not an immediate displacement. The migration from on-premises NGFW to FWaaS requires deliberate planning: firewall policy translation, rule set rationalisation, and an operational model shift as enforcement moves from local appliances to a cloud-managed policy engine. For greenfield deployments or organisations facing imminent hardware refresh, FWaaS is the natural starting point.

What ZTNA and FWaaS Cannot Do Alone

  • SaaS and internet-bound traffic

    ZTNA governs access to private applications. FWaaS governs network-level traffic policy. Neither governs outbound internet access for users or visibility into SaaS application usage. SWG and CASB address those access patterns — the unified model requires all four components to cover the full access surface.

  • Continuous endpoint security

    ZTNA verifies device posture at the point of access. It consumes the compliance signal that MDM and EDR provide — it does not generate it. Continuous endpoint monitoring, threat detection, and response capability remain the domain of EDR and XDR. A compromised device that passes a point-in-time posture check at access time may deteriorate after access is granted. Continuous endpoint monitoring is the control that addresses that gap.

  • East-west workload inspection at depth

    FWaaS enforces network-level segmentation between defined segments. It controls what can communicate with what. It does not provide deep application-layer inspection of east-west traffic between internal workloads — that level of micro-segmentation and workload inspection extends beyond FWaaS into dedicated workload security controls. For most organisations at this scale, FWaaS-level segmentation is the appropriate and proportionate starting point.

RBI and Browser Security

Isolation and enforcement at the browser execution layer — where network controls end and endpoint execution risk begins.

ZT Pillar coverage: Device · Application · Data (execution layer)

Every SSE component discussed so far operates in the traffic path — between the endpoint and the destination. SWG inspects outbound traffic. CASB governs SaaS application sessions. ZTNA controls which applications a user can reach. All of these controls operate at the network and access layer. None of them governs what happens when content actually executes in the browser on the endpoint.

RBI and browser security address that execution layer. They are the controls for two related but distinct problems: RBI addresses scenarios where the device cannot be trusted — the execution environment itself must be moved off the endpoint entirely. Browser security addresses the managed device population — hardening and governing the browser as the primary enterprise application delivery surface.

The Isolation Model

In the standard browser execution model, content from a remote server executes locally. JavaScript runs in the local browser engine. Files download to the local filesystem. A malicious page that survives network-layer inspection reaches the endpoint and executes there. The security of that execution depends entirely on the state of the local device.

RBI removes the local device from the execution path entirely. The browser session runs inside a cloud-hosted container operated by the SSE vendor. The container renders the session output and streams a visual representation to the user's browser. The user interacts with what they see — the interactions are sent back to the container, which acts on them in the remote environment. The remote site communicates only with the container, not with the user's device.

Cloud-delivered RBI implements what NIST SP 800-207 describes as Device Application Sandboxing — the model in which an application runs in a protected compartment, isolated from the potentially compromised host and from other applications. In NIST's framing, the sandboxed application communicates with the Policy Enforcement Point to request access to resources, while the PEP refuses requests from other applications on the same asset. Cloud RBI delivers this at the network layer: the browser session executes in a remote cloud container rather than a local sandbox, with the isolation goal identical — the application cannot compromise the host, and the host cannot compromise the session.

RBI Operates in Two Distinct Deployment Configurations

The term "RBI" covers two meaningfully different architectural decisions. Understanding which configuration is being deployed — and why — determines the policy design, the user experience implications, and the cost model.

Configuration A
Destination-triggered RBI
SWG policy condition · Applied by destination category

The trigger is the URL or destination category. When any user — on any device, managed or unmanaged — accesses an uncategorised or high-risk URL, the session is isolated. The isolation decision is about the destination, not the user or device.

This is the correct Zero Trust policy expression: trust cannot be established for the destination, therefore isolation is the access mechanism. Session-based, condition-triggered, applies across the user population based on what is being accessed rather than who is accessing it.

Configuration B
Device-path RBI
Access architecture decision · Applied by device type

The trigger is the access path. When an unmanaged device user needs access to a higher-sensitivity corporate application or resource, RBI provides the execution isolation layer that prevents data from reaching the unmanaged device.

The isolation decision here is about the device and access architecture, not the destination. This is an architectural decision about how to serve a specific user population — contractors, partners, BYOD users — where managed device requirements cannot be met.

Both configurations use the same cloud container execution mechanism. The policy trigger is different. They are complementary uses of the same capability, not contradictory deployment models. An organisation can run both simultaneously — destination-triggered isolation for uncategorised URLs across all users, and device-path isolation for unmanaged device access to specific sensitive applications.

RBI as a Policy Condition

RBI is not deployed as a per-user control — it is triggered by a policy condition. The SWG evaluates every outbound request against its category database. For requests where trust can be established — known-safe destinations, categorised and permitted sites — the SWG allows the traffic through its standard enforcement path. For requests where trust cannot be determined — uncategorised destinations, unknown risk profile — the policy decision is not simply allow or block. It is isolate.

RBI is a resource-intensive control. Each isolated session requires a cloud container with rendering compute and streaming bandwidth. The cost scales with concurrent active sessions, not with the number of licensed users. This is why condition-triggered deployment — scoped to uncategorised destinations, specific high-risk categories, or defined access patterns — is the operationally sound model.

Licensing — verify before designing

RBI is typically a premium add-on in the SSE licensing stack — not included in base license tiers. Pricing is session-based: costs scale with the volume of concurrent isolated sessions, not with a per-user flat fee. This cost model directly reinforces the recommendation for selective, condition-triggered deployment rather than routing all web traffic through RBI. Verify what the current or planned SSE license tier covers — and at what session volume pricing applies — before designing the RBI deployment.

Advanced RBI features — maximum isolation fidelity through pixel-pushing for all sessions, unlimited concurrent sessions, full inline DLP within isolated sessions — may require a higher license tier or add-on. Evaluate these against the specific use case that requires them.

Why RBI Is the Right Access Model for Contractors and Third Parties

A question that arises naturally when browser security controls are discussed for contractor and third-party access is whether an enterprise browser — a managed browser application or extension with corporate policy enforcement — addresses that population. The practical answer is that it does not, in the general case.

An enterprise browser requires installation on the endpoint. For a contractor working on a device owned and managed by their own employer, installation of enterprise software raises immediate and legitimate obstacles. The device belongs to another organisation. That organisation's IT policy may explicitly prohibit installation of third-party managed software. The contractor may lack administrative rights. And even where installation is technically possible, the contractor's employer must consent to a third party deploying managed, policy-enforced software on hardware they own and are responsible for. In practice, many organisations will decline that request.

There is a more fundamental concern beyond installation logistics. An enterprise browser with session monitoring, DLP, and policy enforcement has visibility into activity within that browser. If the contractor uses the same browser for both the engagement with the hiring organisation and their own employer's work — or other client engagements — the monitoring scope extends beyond the intended boundary. This is a legal and contractual exposure for the deploying organisation, and a legitimate reason for a contractor's employer to refuse consent.

RBI — privacy boundary for contractor access

The container executes the session. The hiring organisation has visibility into what happens inside the container — the sites accessed, data transfers attempted, application interactions within the session. The contractor's device is entirely outside the visibility boundary. No installation required on the contractor's device. The contractor's own employer has nothing to consent to.

Enterprise browser — why it does not fit the contractor model

The managed browser runs on the contractor's device. The hiring organisation's policy enforcement has visibility into activity within that browser. If the same browser is used for multiple purposes, that visibility extends beyond the intended scope. Requires installation on a device the hiring organisation does not own, and consent from an organisation with its own IT governance obligations.

RBI requires nothing on the contractor's device. The contractor opens their existing browser, authenticates through the IdP, and the session executes in the cloud container. For third-party access to higher-sensitivity applications where the application portal's thinner enforcement is insufficient, RBI is the appropriate answer.

What RBI Cannot Do

  • It does not eliminate the human factor

    RBI removes the technical data transfer paths that make browser-based exfiltration straightforward — clipboard pass-through, file download, print-to-file. For insider threat scenarios involving deliberate exfiltration by a motivated individual, RBI raises the operational difficulty without providing an absolute barrier. It is a proportionate and effective technical control — not a complete answer to insider risk.

  • Latency is a real constraint

    The rendering and streaming overhead introduces latency that varies with PoP proximity, network conditions, and the rendering approach deployed. For latency-sensitive workflows — real-time financial applications, interactive data platforms, complex web applications with high interactivity requirements — RBI may degrade the user experience to the point of impracticality. Selective deployment for defined use cases and URL categories is the proportionate model.

  • Non-browser access paths are outside the boundary

    RBI isolates browser sessions. Data exfiltration through desktop synchronisation clients, email attachments, removable media, or direct API access to SaaS platforms is entirely outside the RBI boundary. Endpoint DLP and CASB address those paths. RBI is one component of a layered data protection posture — not a comprehensive answer to data exfiltration risk across all access channels.

Browser Security: The Managed Device Complement

RBI addresses the scenarios where device trust is absent. Browser security addresses the managed device population — the corporate employees on managed, compliant devices where the SSE agent is deployed and the full enforcement stack is in place. Even in that environment, there is a category of risk that network-layer controls cannot reach: what happens within the browser execution environment on the managed device itself.

The browser is the primary enterprise application delivery surface. SaaS applications, internal web tools, cloud development environments, and collaboration platforms all run in the browser. It is also a significant attack surface — phishing pages execute in the browser, malicious extensions have access to everything the user does within it, session tokens are stored in browser memory, and credentials are entered through browser form fields.

One indicator of the direction of travel: Palo Alto Networks integrated an enterprise browser (Prisma Access Browser) directly into their SSE platform — illustrating that the boundary between enterprise browser and SSE is actively dissolving for at least some vendors. The separate enterprise browser product and the SSE agent are converging into a unified endpoint enforcement model for those who have made that architectural choice. Understanding whether the shortlisted SSE vendor has taken this path — or treats enterprise browser as a separate product — is a relevant evaluation criterion.

Session Enclave and three enforcement mechanisms

Enterprise browsers introduce a Session Enclave — a logically isolated container within the browser in which corporate web applications run, separated from the local OS, local storage, and other personal browsers on the same device. Data from the corporate application does not leave the enclave context without policy enforcement. For contractor and BYOD scenarios where installation can be consented to, the Session Enclave addresses the privacy concern by limiting corporate visibility to the enclave context — not to the device as a whole.

At the enforcement mechanism level, enterprise browsers operate through three distinct capabilities that SSE network-layer controls cannot replicate:

Runtime script monitoring and DOM shielding

Monitors JavaScript execution in real time and detects DOM manipulation before network egress. Catches client-side injection attacks invisible to SSE network-layer inspection — the attack executes within the browser session, not in the traffic path between the browser and the destination. This is the enforcement layer that addresses threats operating entirely within the browser sandbox.

Extension sandboxing and permission stripping

Zero-trust extension policies with allowlisting; dangerous runtime permissions stripped even from approved extensions. Browser extensions are a significant and underestimated risk vector — a malicious or compromised extension has access to credentials, session tokens, form data, and all page content visible in the browser. Enterprise browser management enables systematic extension governance that most organisations at this scale do not apply through any other control.

Integrated RBI

Enterprise browsers route high-risk or uncategorised sessions through cloud RBI containers as an integrated protection layer. The enterprise browser and cloud RBI are not alternatives in the advanced deployment model — they are combined. The enterprise browser handles managed device sessions with in-session enforcement; RBI provides the container execution isolation for sessions where destination trust cannot be established. Note: an enterprise browser with runtime monitoring and extension sandboxing does not provide the same execution isolation guarantee as cloud RBI. Content still executes locally within the enterprise browser — the isolation is logical rather than physical. For zero-day exploits targeting the browser engine itself, cloud RBI provides the stronger isolation guarantee.

Credential protection and in-browser DLP

Browser-level controls prevent credential entry on known phishing sites, block password autofill on untrusted domains, and detect when credentials are entered on destinations that do not match the expected legitimate site. In-browser DLP enforces policy within the browser itself — preventing copy-paste of content from sensitive applications to unsanctioned destinations, blocking upload of specific content types from within application sessions, and applying watermarks to sensitive page content. A lighter-weight data protection layer for scenarios where SSE-layer DLP does not reach within-browser interactions.

SSE agents can distinguish traffic by process on the endpoint — steering traffic from the corporate browser through the SSE enforcement path while allowing personal browser traffic to flow differently. This provides a degree of session isolation at the network layer. It does not, however, govern what happens inside the browser session — DOM manipulation, extension behaviour, and in-session data interactions remain outside the SSE agent's visibility. This is where enterprise browser's engine-level instrumentation adds enforcement that SSE traffic-layer controls cannot provide.

The Decision Framework: Enterprise Browser, SSE, or Both

Enterprise browser and SSE are not competing alternatives. They address different layers of the security stack and serve different use cases. Understanding when each is appropriate — and when both are warranted — prevents both over-investment and under-coverage.

Enterprise browser — without full SSE

Browser-primary risk profile
  • Risk is primarily browser and SaaS-based — no significant private application access requirements, no branch office complexity, no non-browser traffic concerns
  • Risk is primarily in-browser in-session threats: client-side injection, extension abuse, DOM manipulation, credential harvesting
  • SaaS-centric workforce where faster deployment path is a priority — enterprise browser deploys in minutes without network rerouting or endpoint agent rollout
  • User population is on corporate-issued or BYOD managed devices where browser installation is feasible and the employment relationship establishes consent
Honest boundary: enterprise browser governs the browsing session. It does not govern private application access, outbound non-browser traffic, branch connectivity, or any access pattern that falls outside the browser. Organisations with these requirements need SSE.

SSE — without enterprise browser

Full access surface coverage
  • Full coverage required across SWG, CASB, ZTNA, DLP, and network-level policy — the complete access surface, not only the browser
  • Managed device population can accept the SSE endpoint agent — single enforcement stack with one policy console is operationally preferable
  • Private application access, branch office connectivity, or non-browser traffic types are in scope
  • SSE agent per-process traffic steering meets per-application enforcement needs for the managed device fleet
  • RBI within the SSE platform addresses isolation scenarios where required — no separate browser product needed for those cases
Honest boundary: SSE's network-layer controls do not reach within-browser interactions — extension activity, in-browser copy-paste, credential entry events. For organisations where those specific risks are significant, browser security adds a layer SSE alone cannot provide.

Both SSE and enterprise browser

Layered enforcement model
  • SSE provides the primary enforcement stack for managed devices across all traffic types — SWG, CASB, ZTNA, DLP through the endpoint agent
  • Enterprise browser adds session-layer enforcement SSE cannot reach: DOM inspection, extension governance, in-session DLP for managed device population
  • Organisation has a meaningful BYOD population on personal devices where a managed corporate browser profile is feasible and consented — the Session Enclave model addresses privacy concerns for that population
  • Contractor and unmanaged device access uses the application portal and RBI — enterprise browser is not appropriate for devices the organisation does not own or control, for the installation and privacy reasons described above
Honest caution: enterprise browser is not a substitute for SSE. Deploying enterprise browser instead of SSE leaves the non-browser risk surface — private application access, outbound traffic control, branch connectivity, data in transit — entirely ungoverned. The correct model is SSE as the foundation, with enterprise browser as an additive layer for within-browser enforcement gaps.

SSE DLP

Data protection at the Policy Enforcement Point — governing data in motion at the SSE enforcement layer when every upstream access control has said yes.

ZT Pillar coverage: Data

Every component in the SSE stack discussed so far governs access. Identity controls who is permitted. Device posture controls what they access from. ZTNA controls which applications they can reach. SWG controls which internet destinations they can visit. CASB governs what they do within SaaS applications. RBI controls where content executes. All of these controls operate on the access decision — can this subject reach this resource through this path.

SSE DLP operates on a different question: given that the access has been permitted, what data is leaving through that permitted path?

The scenario that makes this distinction concrete: a user is authorised to access a customer database. Their credentials are valid, their device is compliant, their ZTNA access is permitted, and the SWG has allowed the session to the relevant application. CASB sees an authenticated session to a sanctioned platform. Every access control in the stack said yes. The user then downloads a large dataset and uploads it to a personal cloud storage account.

Before reaching SSE DLP, the first question is whether CASB with upload controls and tenant awareness should have caught that upload. If the destination is a personal OneDrive or Dropbox — same platform, different tenant — inline CASB with tenant awareness enforcement should be the primary control. SSE DLP at the egress layer is the secondary and residual control: the layer that catches what CASB may miss — uploads to uncategorised or newly created destinations, non-browser egress paths, content that matches data sensitivity criteria but not CASB application-level controls. That is why SSE DLP is the last line of enforcement, not the first.

Data Classification: The Foundation Layer

SSE DLP policy enforces rules against defined sensitivity categories. A rule that blocks upload of documents classified as Confidential requires a Confidential classification to exist — applied to documents, maintained across the estate, and recognisable to the DLP engine. Without that foundation, the DLP policy can only enforce pattern-based rules for well-understood data types.

SSE-native classification

Every SSE platform includes pre-built policy templates for regulated data categories — PII formats, PCI-DSS cardholder data, HIPAA-relevant health information, GDPR-covered personal data types, and common financial data patterns. These templates are deployable immediately without external classification infrastructure.

As an example of how SSE DLP licensing works in practice: Palo Alto Networks bundles SaaS Security Inline, SaaS Security API, and Enterprise DLP as components within their CASB-X license tier. Understanding what is bundled versus what requires a higher tier — for any vendor — is the licence verification discipline this guide recommends before deployment.

Even pre-built templates require tuning before enforcement mode. A credit card number regex fires on any 16-digit string matching the format — including test data, internal account numbers, and PO numbers. A HIPAA template fires on any document containing health-related terminology including company health and safety policies and HR benefits documents. The alert-only tuning period identifies these false positive categories before enforcement is activated.

Right starting point: deploy built-in templates on day one in alert-only mode, tune for two to four weeks, layer organisation-specific classification as the taxonomy matures.
External classification integration

Organisations with a mature data classification programme can connect that classification intelligence to the SSE enforcement layer. As of November 2025, Microsoft Purview DLP policies integrate with SASE platforms — including Netskope and iboss — and with Entra Global Secure Access to enable inline network traffic inspection and enforcement based on Purview DLP policy conditions.

For Microsoft-ecosystem organisations, this changes the displacement question materially: the SSE vendor's DLP engine may be partially displaceable by Purview for file-based network egress control. The SSE DLP engine still adds value for non-Microsoft content, advanced ML classification capabilities beyond Purview's scope, and egress paths not covered by the Purview-integrated enforcement point.

Purview network DLP inspection through Entra Global Secure Access is a pay-as-you-go feature — not included in E5. Billed per request volume. For high-volume environments, the per-request pricing model should be modelled against SSE DLP flat licensing before a decision is made.

For organisations outside the Microsoft ecosystem, SSE-native templates plus EDM registration for known sensitive datasets is the practical path.
The classification prerequisite in practice

Organisations that deploy SSE DLP before establishing a data classification foundation consistently encounter one of two outcomes: DLP policy that is too broad — generating alert volumes the team cannot investigate — or DLP policy that is too narrow — catching only the well-defined patterns while leaving the majority of the sensitive data estate unprotected. The classification foundation is not a parallel workstream. It is the prerequisite that determines whether SSE DLP functions as a security control or as operational overhead.

For EDM at scale: Zscaler's EDM capability illustrates how the mechanism works across major SSE vendors — organisations fingerprint large structured sensitive datasets with only hash signatures stored in the cloud enforcement infrastructure, not the data itself. The data is not uploaded to the vendor; only cryptographic fingerprints are. This is the model most SSE vendors use for EDM, and understanding it removes the concern that sensitive data must be shared with the vendor for fingerprinting to function.

A Maturity-Gated Deployment Approach

Stage 1 Deploy now — no classification required
  • Deploy in alert-only mode first — not enforcement mode. A tuning period of two to four weeks before activating enforcement allows the team to understand actual traffic patterns, identify false positive categories, and establish a baseline before any blocking occurs
  • Activate SSE-native pre-built templates for PII, PCI-DSS, and other regulated data formats
  • Configure pattern matching rules for the organisation's most common regulated data types
  • Begin EDM registration for structured sensitive datasets already inventoried — customer records, employee data. EDM is available in Stage 1 without requiring full data classification infrastructure, and dramatically reduces false positives for that structured data
  • Use alert data to understand actual exfiltration patterns before building broader policy
Prerequisite: none beyond SSE deployment. Activating enforcement without the tuning period produces unpredictable alert volumes and pressure to disable the policy.
Stage 2 Extend — once classification foundation is ready
  • Connect sensitivity label framework (Purview or equivalent) to SSE enforcement layer
  • Build classification-based DLP rules against the organisation's defined sensitivity taxonomy
  • Enable ML-based classification for unstructured content — allow a dedicated tuning period before enforcement actions are activated
  • Move high-confidence rules from alert to block as false-positive rates are established
  • Extend EDM coverage to additional sensitive data assets as inventory is completed
Prerequisite: sensitivity labels deployed and actively maintained across the document estate. This is a maturity gate — Stage 2 activates when the classification foundation is ready, which may be months after Stage 1 is in production.
Plan for the tuning phase

A DLP deployment without an explicit tuning phase — defined as a period where alerts are reviewed, policy is refined, and false-positive categories are identified and addressed before enforcement actions are enabled — consistently produces alert fatigue, policy loosening, and a programme that exists on paper but functions as an opt-in control. The tuning phase should be planned as part of the deployment programme, not treated as an optional post-deployment activity.

What SSE DLP Cannot Do Alone

  • It cannot protect what has not been classified

    SSE DLP enforces policy against defined categories and registered data assets. Sensitive data that carries no classification label, matches no registered pattern, and falls outside the ML model's training scope is invisible to the DLP engine. The quality of the DLP programme is directly bounded by the quality of the classification foundation beneath it.

  • It cannot inspect traffic it cannot decrypt

    Traffic in the TLS bypass list is outside SSE DLP's visibility. This is the deliberate consequence of the decryption governance decisions discussed in Section 3. This is an architectural boundary that must be understood when scoping what the DLP programme protects and communicating that scope accurately to stakeholders.

  • It does not address insider intent

    SSE DLP detects and blocks specific technical exfiltration paths through the web egress layer. A determined insider who understands the policy can route around it — using permitted channels, breaking sensitive content into fragments below detection thresholds, or using personal devices on personal networks. SSE DLP is a proportionate and effective control against opportunistic and accidental data loss. It is not a comprehensive answer to insider threat.

  • It does not replace CASB for SaaS-internal data movement

    SSE DLP inspects content in the outbound web egress path. Data moving within a SaaS application — shared links, internal permissions changes, data moved between services via native API integrations — does not traverse the outbound path that SSE DLP inspects. API CASB is the control that addresses data governance within SaaS applications. SSE DLP and CASB are complementary layers.

Key Evaluation Questions

Key evaluation questions

What does your Endpoint DLP actually cover?

If Endpoint DLP is deployed, the relevant question is what access paths it governs. Modern Endpoint DLP implementations can cover browser-based upload paths through browser monitoring or kernel-level file operation interception. If browser uploads are within scope, the residual gap that SSE DLP addresses is narrower and more specific. This is a configuration audit question that determines whether SSE DLP closes a meaningful gap or largely duplicates existing coverage.

Do you have a mature Purview deployment with sensitivity labels actively maintained?

If yes — and if you are already using or plan to use Netskope, iboss, or Entra Global Secure Access — Purview's network DLP integration (generally available November 2025) may address file-based egress control without requiring the SSE vendor's DLP module. Assess the per-request pricing model against expected egress inspection volume before committing to the SSE DLP license. The gap that SSE DLP addresses in this scenario is non-Microsoft egress paths and content that carries no label despite being sensitive.

Is the team capacity to manage DLP alerts realistic?

A team of two managing a DLP programme producing several hundred alerts per week will either invest the majority of available security operations time in DLP triage or will deprioritise the alerts, at which point the DLP programme exists operationally in name only. A narrower, well-tuned programme that the team can actually action produces better security outcomes than a broad programme generating noise that accumulates unreviewed.

Recommended Implementation Sequence

A maturity-gated deployment path — maximum risk reduction first, minimum early complexity, prerequisite dependencies respected.

The governing principle

"Deploy what reduces the most significant risk first, with the least operational complexity, while respecting prerequisite dependencies."

The preceding sections addressed each SSE component individually. The reader now understands what each control does, where it fits in the unified model, and what existing investments may already cover. The question this raises is practical: in what order should they be deployed?

The answer is not "deploy everything simultaneously." That produces maximum complexity, maximum cost, and maximum operational burden from day one — with a lean team managing more change than can be absorbed, debugged, or tuned concurrently. Nor is it "follow the vendor's recommended deployment path." Vendors have a legitimate interest in getting all components active quickly. That interest is not always aligned with what produces the best security outcomes for a two or three person security team.

On organisational entry points

This sequence is a recommended starting point, not a universal prescription. The existing investment audit from Section 2 determines where an individual organisation enters. An organisation with mature Conditional Access, broad SSO coverage, and an established device management programme enters at a different point than one building identity and endpoint foundations from scratch. Read the sequence as the logical order — identify where your current state places you — and begin there.

Each step in the sequence delivers a meaningful security outcome independently. The sequence has real dependency constraints: inline SSE components — inline CASB, inline DLP, and RBI — depend on the SSE agent being deployed and traffic steered through the enforcement platform. The SWG deployment in Step 3 establishes the traffic steering and enforcement path that all subsequent inline components use. API-based CASB — discovery mode, shadow IT analysis, OAuth governance — is the genuine exception: it connects directly to SaaS APIs and can operate before the SSE agent is deployed. Steps that do not depend on the SSE enforcement path — identity foundation, DNS filtering, ZTNA connector deployment — can proceed in parallel with the SWG deployment.

The nine steps above are presented in logical order. Several can run in parallel — they are not all strictly sequential. The most common sequencing mistake

Activating enforcement policies before traffic coverage is confirmed and before the traffic landscape is understood through an observation period. The result: enforcement actions fire against an incomplete picture of actual traffic, producing either excessive false positives from legitimate traffic the policy did not anticipate, or enforcement gaps where traffic bypasses the enforcement point entirely. The observation period in Steps 3 and 4 is not a delay — it is the investment that makes enforcement effective when it is activated.

Platform Cohesion and Operational Resilience

The resilience argument

A security platform is only as resilient as the team's ability to maintain full operational visibility across it, respond to incidents within it, and tune enforcement policy within it. For a lean security team, resilience is proportional to depth of understanding — and depth of understanding is proportional to the number of systems that must be mastered simultaneously.

A best-of-breed stack requires integration work between platforms, multiple policy consoles, multiple support relationships, and operational expertise distributed across multiple products. Each integration point is a potential enforcement gap. Each console is a context switch during an incident. When a DLP alert fires at 11pm and the on-call engineer must correlate it across three separate platforms with three separate logging formats, the fragmentation is not a management preference problem — it is a security outcome problem.

A cohesive single-vendor platform that the team can fully understand, tune, and respond to is a more resilient platform than a best-of-breed stack that exceeds the team's operational capacity. The capability trade-off is real — no single vendor is best-of-breed across every component. The resilience trade-off is also real, and for a lean team, it consistently outweighs the marginal capability gain from best-of-breed selection.

PoP Geography, Full-Stack Enforcement, and Data Protection Obligations

PoP geography is a necessary but insufficient evaluation criterion. The question is not only whether a PoP exists in a geography, but whether the full SSE enforcement stack executes at that PoP — and what data protection obligations apply to the processing that occurs there.

Data protection obligation verification — ask every shortlisted vendor

When traffic inspection occurs at a vendor's PoP infrastructure, three distinct data protection dimensions apply — all require verification, not assumption.

Data processing location. Where does the vendor's infrastructure actually process the decrypted traffic? A PoP that steers traffic but forwards inspection to regional infrastructure in a different geography does not mean inspection occurs locally. For regulated organisations, the processing location — not just the PoP location — determines which jurisdiction's laws govern the data during inspection.

Data sovereignty. Which legal jurisdiction's laws apply to the data during processing? A PoP geographically within the EU does not automatically mean EU law governs — if the vendor is headquartered outside the EU, other jurisdictions may assert access rights regardless of where the data physically sits. Post-Schrems II, this distinction between physical location and legal jurisdiction is material, particularly for GDPR-regulated data.

Data processor obligations. The SSE vendor is a data processor under GDPR — processing personal data on behalf of the controller. The Data Processing Agreement must accurately describe what data is processed, where, under what conditions, the sub-processor chain, and incident notification obligations. SOC 2 Type II and ISO 27001 certifications establish that the vendor maintains a security programme — they do not provide specific assurance about inspection pipeline architecture or sub-processor geography. Review what is specifically in scope in the vendor's certification reports, not just that the certification exists.

PoP due diligence questions — ask every shortlisted vendor
  1. For each PoP in our required geographies, which SSE enforcement functions — specifically SWG inspection, DLP content inspection, ML-based classification, and CASB policy evaluation — execute locally at that PoP, and which are forwarded to regional or central infrastructure for processing?
  2. Can you provide contractual commitment in the Data Processing Agreement that DLP inspection and processing of our traffic will not occur outside our specified geographic boundaries — and that this commitment survives changes to your infrastructure?
  3. What is your sub-processor list for the geographies relevant to our regulatory obligations, and are those sub-processors bound by the same data processing commitments as your primary contract?
  4. If new PoPs are opened or inspection functions are relocated, what is your notification process, and do we have contractual recourse if data protection commitments are breached?
  5. Under which legal jurisdiction's laws is traffic processed at each relevant PoP — and does your legal structure create potential for foreign government access rights to apply to that processing regardless of physical location?

Professional services and deployment support

A lean team deploying a complex SSE platform for the first time benefits from structured deployment support. During the sales process, evaluate what deployment support is included in the base contract versus what requires additional purchase — and assess whether the vendor's deployment programme is calibrated for a mid-size organisation or designed for enterprise-scale deployments with dedicated programme management resources.

Support model

Confirm the support tier included in the base contract, the response time commitments for critical and high-severity incidents, and the escalation path when first-line support cannot resolve the issue. Test the support model during the PoC — open a non-trivial technical question and assess response quality and time before committing.

Release cadence and change management

Cloud-delivered SSE platforms are updated continuously. Understand the vendor's process for introducing breaking changes, how new features are communicated before deployment, whether the organisation has control over when agent updates are applied to the managed device fleet, and what the rollback procedure is when an update introduces unexpected behaviour.

What Vendor Selection Is Not

  • ✕

    It is not an analyst ranking exercise

    Market analyst quadrants reflect a vendor's overall market position, sales execution, and product completeness across a broad population of enterprise customers. They do not reflect fit for a specific mid-size organisation with a specific risk profile, existing investment landscape, and team scale. Analyst rankings confirm that a vendor is credible. They are not a selection tool.

  • ✕

    It is not a feature comparison spreadsheet

    Every major SASE vendor will check every feature box on a standard RFP feature list. The differentiating questions are not "do you support X feature" — they are "how does your implementation of X work with our specific IdP, our specific application estate, and our specific team's operational capacity." A PoC in the actual environment confirms behaviour. No other test does.

  • ✕

    It is not a price optimisation exercise

    The lowest license cost for a platform the team cannot effectively operate, or that does not fit the organisation's dominant risk domain, is not a saving. It is a deferred cost that surfaces as a programme re-platform eighteen months later. Evaluate on fit and total cost, not on license cost alone.

A Practical Evaluation Approach

1Shortlist from audit-derived criteria

Apply the hard criteria from the evaluation table. Vendors that fail a hard criterion are removed before evaluation begins. Aim for two to three vendors on the shortlist. More than three produces evaluation overhead that exceeds the signal gained.

2PoC in the actual environment

Always recommended. Not a vendor-demo environment. A PoC using the organisation's actual managed devices, the actual IdP, a representative sample of private applications published through a connector, and actual traffic steered through the platform. Test the access patterns that matter — the non-HTTP applications, the unmanaged device paths, the DLP scenarios relevant to the actual data estate.

3Operational assessment during PoC

The team that deploys the PoC is the team that will operate the production platform. Evaluate: how quickly can a policy change be made and validated? How clear are the logs when an enforcement action fires unexpectedly? How responsive is the support team when a PoC issue is raised? These answers predict operational resilience during production incidents.

On the PoC and vendor engagement

A vendor that resists a PoC in the actual environment — preferring instead a managed demo environment or a reference architecture walkthrough — is signalling something about their confidence in the platform's behaviour outside controlled conditions. A vendor that engages the PoC seriously, assigns capable technical resources, and responds quickly to issues encountered is demonstrating the operational model the organisation will experience in production. Both signals are informative.

Vendor selection closes the programme design decisions. The guide closes in Section 10 by looking beyond the current deployment — at the surface areas the Zero Trust and SASE model described in this guide needs to extend toward as the technology and threat environment evolves. The foundation built through Sections 1 to 9 is the right starting point for what comes next.

Section 10 Zero Trust, Delivered — A Practitioner's Guide to SASE as Your Implementation Engine

The Expanding Surface

Governance sustains the programme. The surface continues to grow beyond what the programme currently governs.

The programme decisions described in this guide are complete. The enforcement stack is deployed or in deployment. SWG, CASB, ZTNA, RBI, SSE DLP, and FWaaS cover the established domain — users and endpoints accessing applications, SaaS platforms, and internet destinations through governed access paths. That is genuine, meaningful security improvement.

Two things are true simultaneously. The programme the reader has built represents the right foundation. And the surface that foundation must govern is not static — it was always broader than users and endpoints, and it continues to expand. The question for this section is not what has been built, but what sustains it and what comes next.

The Governance Function: Sustaining the Programme

The enforcement stack creates the technical controls. The implementation sequence establishes them in the right order. The vendor selection provides the platform. None of those things — on their own — keep the programme working well over time. The mechanism that does is governance: a structured function, separate from the deployment and operational team, that reviews policy, manages exceptions, assesses risk, and tracks programme maturity on an ongoing basis.

The degradation problem
Zero Trust programmes erode when no one is accountable for the whole picture.

The enforcement stack does not degrade on its own. The policy set does. Each individual exception has a justification. A legacy application needs a VPN bypass until it is migrated — but migration is deprioritised and the bypass persists for three years. A ZTNA policy scoped broadly "to get things working" is never tightened after the initial deployment pressure subsides. A DLP rule generates too many false positives and is quietly disabled rather than tuned. A contractor who left six months ago still has an active portal access account.

None of these decisions is individually dramatic. Cumulatively, they produce a programme that looks like Zero Trust on paper and operates like a network-trust model in practice. The enforcement stack enforces the policy. Governance ensures the policy remains aligned with Zero Trust principles over time.

The pattern through which Zero Trust programmes typically erode follows a consistent sequence:

STAGE 1
Exception created
A bypass, a broad policy scope, or an access grant is created with a legitimate justification and no expiry
STAGE 2
Exception normalised
The exception persists past its original justification. No one challenges it because it is not causing visible problems
STAGE 3
Exceptions multiply
The pattern repeats. New exceptions are created on the same basis. Each has a justification. No one tracks the cumulative effect
STAGE 4
Policy drift
The effective policy set no longer reflects the intended Zero Trust model. The gap between design and reality is invisible until an incident reveals it

What the governance function does

Policy review

Every new policy request is assessed before implementation: does it enforce least-privilege, or does it default to broad access for operational convenience? Does it create or expand attack surface? Does it conflict with existing policies in ways that produce gaps? The governance function holds the whole-programme view that the operational team, focused on individual requests, cannot maintain.

Exception management

Every exception to the Zero Trust model — a bypass, a policy exclusion, a connector with broad scope, a rule disabled for operational reasons — must have a documented business justification, a named owner, an expiry date, and a review cycle. Exceptions without these attributes are not managed exceptions. They are ungoverned gaps. The governance function maintains the exception register and enforces this discipline.

Risk assessment for policy changes

A change that appears minor in isolation — extending a ZTNA access scope, adding a destination to the DLP bypass list, broadening a CASB allowlist — does two things simultaneously: it creates a potential enforcement gap, and it expands the attack surface. The governance function evaluates both dimensions for every change request. What has been made accessible that was not before? What has been made uninspectable that was inspected before? Both questions require documented, justified answers before a policy change is approved. The cumulative attack surface effect of policy drift — individually justified exceptions accumulating into a materially weakened posture — is what the governance function exists to prevent.

Periodic policy audit

The access policy set accumulates policy debris over time — ZTNA rules for applications that no longer exist, access grants for users who have left, DLP rules built for vendors that were replaced. A quarterly or semi-annual audit cycle identifies and removes debris that increases operational complexity and attack surface without providing security value.

Exception register

A structured log of all active exceptions to the Zero Trust model. Each entry records what the exception is, why it exists, who owns it, when it was approved, and when it expires. The register is reviewed at each governance cycle. Expired exceptions are either renewed with fresh justification or removed. The register makes the real policy posture visible — not the intended posture, but what is actually in effect.

Maturity tracking and board reporting

The governance function tracks programme maturity against the Zero Trust framework, identifies gaps, and prioritises remediation. This is the input to CISO and board-level reporting — not a vendor dashboard screenshot, but a structured assessment of where the programme stands, what the residual gaps are, and what the prioritised roadmap looks like.

What the governance function looks like at this scale

For a mid-size organisation, the governance function is unlikely to be a dedicated team. It is a defined process with a named owner and a regular cadence — not a headcount requirement but a function requirement. A monthly review meeting with a defined agenda, a documented exception register, and a clear escalation path to the CISO or security steering group constitutes a meaningful governance function. The mechanism matters. The meeting cadence matters. The register matters.

The exception register — minimum required fields

An exception register does not need to be complex. The minimum: exception description, affected component (SWG bypass / ZTNA scope / DLP rule / CASB allowlist), business justification, risk assessment (what does this exception expose, and how does it expand the attack surface?), named owner, approval date, expiry date, and review cycle. An exception without an expiry date is not a managed exception — it is a permanent gap waiting to be discovered.

Expansion 1 — OT/IoT: The Device Without Identity

Surface expansion 01
The device without identity
Present now, growing with every connected device added to the estate

Zero Trust was designed around a subject that can authenticate. Users authenticate through an IdP. Managed devices authenticate through certificates and compliance signals. The entire SSE enforcement model assumes a subject with an identity that can be verified continuously.

OT and IoT devices cannot authenticate in this sense. A manufacturing controller, a building management system, an IP camera, a networked printer, a medical device — these have no IdP integration, no user identity, no posture agent. They communicate through protocols designed for reliability and determinism, not for identity-based access control.

The Zero Trust approach for OT/IoT shifts from identity-based access control to network-based access control. The subject cannot authenticate, so the control shifts to what it can communicate with and what can communicate with it. FWaaS-enforced network segmentation, introduced in Section 5, is where this control is applied — isolating OT/IoT device segments from corporate user networks, governing permitted communication paths, and preventing lateral movement from a compromised device into the user environment.

Discover before you segment. Most organisations do not have an accurate inventory of OT/IoT devices on their network. Passive monitoring tools — purpose-built to listen to OT protocol traffic and build device inventories without touching the devices — are the discovery instrument. Attempting segmentation without an accurate device inventory produces over-blocking of legitimate OT communications or under-blocking that leaves segmentation gaps.

Monitoring as the operational control. Because OT/IoT devices cannot run agents and cannot be inspected through SSE controls, passive monitoring of their traffic patterns is the primary anomaly detection mechanism. A device that initiates connections to destinations outside its normal communication profile is the detection signal. Specialised OT security monitoring tools — not SSE components — provide this capability. SASE governs network access control around OT/IoT. OT-specific threat detection requires dedicated tooling at a separate layer.

Expansion 2 — The Broader Zero Trust Picture

Surface expansion 02
Complementary enforcement layers
SASE is one layer — not the whole architecture

A complete Zero Trust posture requires understanding which access patterns fall within the SASE enforcement layer and which require complementary tooling at a different layer. SASE is not a universal Zero Trust platform. It is the enforcement layer for user and device access.

User and endpoint access to APIs and modern application architectures — this is within SASE scope. When a user on a managed endpoint calls an internal API, that access resolves to a hostname or URL governed by ZTNA or SWG. The evolving challenge is the precision of API-specific policy — SSE platforms are actively developing this capability, and dedicated API gateway controls provide more granular depth for organisations with complex API security requirements.

East-west workload-to-workload traffic — this has never been in the SASE domain, and it is not a SASE limitation. Server-to-server communication within a data centre or cloud environment is east-west traffic governed by micro-segmentation tools. Platforms such as Illumio, Akamai Guardicore, VMware NSX, and cloud-native equivalents address lateral movement prevention, workload-level policy enforcement, and breach containment within the internal network. These are complementary Zero Trust disciplines — distinct tools with distinct purposes, operating at a different layer from SASE.

Service identity and cloud IAM — cloud-native IAM platforms govern service-to-service authentication within cloud environments. When a cloud service accesses another cloud service, the trust decision is made by the cloud IAM platform — not by the SASE enforcement layer. Applying least-privilege principles to service identities — scoping them to the minimum permissions required, reviewing them periodically, detecting anomalous usage — is a governance discipline that mirrors what this guide has described for user access, applied at the workload layer.

The three layers together constitute a comprehensive Zero Trust coverage model. Each is a distinct investment with distinct tooling. None replaces the others.

SASE / SSE
User + device access

Governs north-south traffic — users and endpoints accessing applications, SaaS platforms, internet destinations, and private resources through the SSE enforcement stack.

Tools: SWG, CASB, ZTNA, RBI, SSE DLP, FWaaS — delivered through the unified SASE platform.

Micro-segmentation
Workload + east-west

Governs east-west traffic — workload-to-workload communication within data centres and cloud environments. Prevents lateral movement, enforces workload-level least-privilege, and contains breaches at the segment boundary.

Tools: Illumio, Akamai Guardicore, VMware NSX, cloud-native VPC security groups and network policies.

Cloud IAM
Service + workload identity

Governs service identity — cloud workloads authenticating to cloud resources through managed identities, service accounts, and IAM role bindings. Applies least-privilege to non-human subjects in cloud-native architectures.

Tools: Azure Managed Identity / Entra Workload ID, AWS IAM, GCP Workload Identity, cloud-native API gateways.

Expansion 3 — Agentic AI: The Agent Without a Principal

Surface expansion 03
The agent without a principal
Present today in every organisation that has deployed AI assistants or workflow automation

An AI agent — a system that takes actions autonomously, calls tools, accesses data, and initiates transactions on behalf of a user or an organisation — creates an access pattern that the current Zero Trust model was not designed to fully address. The agent may operate under the user's identity, a service identity, or an identity created dynamically for the session. It accesses resources that the user is authorised to access. But it may access them in ways, at volumes, or in combinations the user never would — and it may be manipulated through prompt injection, data poisoning, or tool abuse to take actions its operator never intended.

The Zero Trust questions for agentic AI are the same questions asked for any subject — applied to a new class of actor.

Who authorised this action? An agent acting on behalf of a user should be scoped to the minimum permissions required for its defined purpose — not to the full scope of what the user is authorised to do. Least-privilege applies to agents as strictly as it applies to users. An agent with access to all resources a user can reach is an agent with an unbounded and ungoverned blast radius.

What can this agent access? Agent access to tools, APIs, data sources, and external services should be explicitly defined and governed. The governance function described earlier in this section applies directly — agent access grants are exceptions to the baseline access model and require the same documentation, justification, expiry, and review cycle that any other exception requires.

Is this action within expected behaviour? Agents can be manipulated to take actions their authorised scope permits but their operator never intended. Behavioural monitoring — what is the agent doing relative to its defined purpose, and does its pattern of tool calls and data access match expected operation — is the detection mechanism. This is an emerging discipline without mature tooling. Awareness of the risk is the starting point.

Where does the SSE enforcement layer apply? SSE components govern agent-initiated traffic that traverses the web egress path. An agent that uploads data to an external service, calls a SaaS API, or accesses a web resource traverses SWG and CASB enforcement. CASB shadow AI controls from Section 4 extend naturally to agent-initiated SaaS interactions. The enforcement gap is at the workload and API layer — agents that operate through direct cloud API calls or internal service access may not traverse the SSE enforcement path. The governance and identity controls at the cloud IAM layer are the relevant mechanism for those access patterns.

The Unifying Thread

Three surface expansions. One underlying challenge.

OT / IoT

The device without identity. The subject exists and is connected to the network. It cannot authenticate through an IdP. It cannot run a posture agent. Zero Trust enforcement must shift from identity-based to network-based control.

The challenge: governing access for subjects that cannot identify themselves.
Workload identity

The service without a device. The subject has an identity — a managed identity, a service account — but no human or physical endpoint behind it. Zero Trust enforcement shifts from the SSE layer to the cloud IAM and micro-segmentation layers.

The challenge: governing access for subjects that have no device context.
Agentic AI

The agent without a stable principal. The subject acts autonomously, its scope is partially defined and partially emergent, and its behaviour can be influenced by inputs it processes. Zero Trust enforcement must extend into agent orchestration and tool access governance.

The challenge: governing access for subjects whose actions cannot be fully anticipated.

Each expansion requires extending Zero Trust principles into a new layer of the architecture. The enforcement mechanisms differ. But the governing principle is the same one stated in Section 1: no subject, device, or connection is trusted by default. Access is granted based on verified identity, assessed posture, and contextual signals — continuously, not once at login. The surface to which that principle must be applied is expanding. The foundation built through this guide is the right architecture to extend from — and the governance function described in this section is the mechanism that keeps that extension disciplined as it grows.

The organisations that implement this foundation well — unified Zero Trust enforcement across the established domain of users, devices, applications, and data — are building more than a security platform. They are building the operational discipline, the architectural thinking, and the governance maturity that each of these expansions requires. The policy design disciplines instilled by ZTNA deployment apply directly to workload identity governance. The exception management process built for the SSE programme applies directly to agent access governance. The network segmentation thinking applied to OT/IoT applies directly to micro-segmentation of internal workloads.

The organisations that defer this foundation will face the expanding surface on unstable ground — governing new access patterns without the disciplines that make governance effective, and extending a security model that was never properly established in the first place.

The window to build it well is now.
